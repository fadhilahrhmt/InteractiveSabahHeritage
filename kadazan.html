Log_01 // Origins

NUNUK RAGANG

Deeply entwined with the mist-shrouded landscapes of Sabah, the Kadazan people the region's largest indigenous group, claim a lineage born from myth and soil. Their story begins at Nunuk Ragang, a legendary site near the Liwagu River where, beneath the sprawling canopy of a colossal banyan tree, their ancestors are said to have first flourished. This deep connection to the earth defined their history as master wet-paddy farmers in the fertile plains of Penampang and Papar, where every facet of life was dictated by the celestial rhythm of the moon and the life cycle of rice. More than just a means of survival, this agricultural heartbeat forged a society built on the pillars of communal labor and a profound, spiritual harmony with the natural world.

Log_02 // Spiritual Heart

UNDUK NGADAU

Central to Kadazan history is their ancient belief system, which centers on a supreme creator known as Kinorohingan and the sacred rice spirit, Bambarayon. In the past, spiritual life was managed by the Bobohizan, a class of highly respected priestesses who acted as intermediaries between the physical and spirit worlds. These women were the custodians of oral history, memorizing and chanting the Rinait (sacred prayers) during rituals to heal the sick, protect the village, and ensure a bountiful harvest. Their role was vital in preserving the cultural identity of the people through centuries of change. Today, the legacy of the Kadazan is most vibrantly expressed through the Pesta Kaamatan (Harvest Festival) held every May. This celebration is a modern continuation of ancient rites of thanksgiving for the rice harvest. A significant highlight of this festival is the Unduk Ngadau pageant, which commemorates the legendary sacrifice of Huminodun, a maiden who gave her life to save her people from famine.

For the men, the ensemble is known as the Gaung, a long-sleeved black shirt adorned with gold buttons and trimmings, worn with matching trousers called Souva. A critical component of the male attire is the Sigar, a hand-woven headcloth made from dastar fabric. The Siga is not merely a hat; the intricate way it is folded indicates the wearer’s specific district of origin or social standing. Together, these garments and accessories—including the Tangkong (brass ring hip belts)—represent a sophisticated craftsmanship that has been passed down through generations to preserve the visual identity of the Kadazan.

The traditional attire of the Kadazan is globally recognized for its elegant use of black velvet, a fabric choice that traditionally symbolized prestige and provided a stark, beautiful contrast against bright gold trimmings. For women, the attire typically consists of the Sinuangga, a short-sleeved blouse featuring a U-neck and gold embroidery (Siring) along the seams. This is paired with the Tapi, a long cylindrical wrap-around skirt. The most iconic accessory is the Himpogot, a belt made of antique silver coins (British trade dollars), which serves as both a decorative piece and a display of family wealth.
