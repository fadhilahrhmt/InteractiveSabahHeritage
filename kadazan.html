Log_01 // Origins

Oldest indigenous group

The Murut people are one of the oldest indigenous groups in Sabah and mostly live in the inland areas such as Keningau, Tenom, and Nabawan. Traditionally, they lived near rivers and forests and were known as skilled hunters and farmers. In the past, the Murut practiced shifting agriculture and used blowpipes for hunting. Their culture is closely connected to nature, community traditions, and traditional ceremonies that have been passed down for generations.

Log_02 // Spiritual Heart

The Spirit of Murut

The Murut people traditionally believed in spirits and the importance of living in harmony with nature. They believed that spirits existed in forests, rivers, and the surrounding environment. In the past, rituals and ceremonies were carried out to seek protection, healing, and good harvests. Spiritual leaders helped guide the community and preserve Murut traditions and beliefs.Today, the Murut people continue to preserve their culture and traditions while adapting to modern life in Sabah. Many Murut communities still celebrate traditional festivals, perform cultural dances, and wear traditional clothing during special events. Although modern education and technology are now part of daily life, the Murut people remain proud of their heritage and continue to pass their customs and values to younger generations.

he traditional attire for Murut men is a powerful reflection of their warrior heritage and deep connection to the forest, defined by garments crafted from natural materials and intricate symbolic details. The ensemble features the Babaru Puputul, a sleeveless jacket made from processed tapa bark, paired with the Aba Puputul, a matching bark loincloth that serves as the traditional lower garment. To complete the silhouette, men wear the Tupi Sinulatan, a distinctive headgear adorned with the majestic feathers of the Argus pheasant or hornbill, which historically signified the wearer's bravery and social status within the tribe.

These garments are meticulously embellished with tiny, colorful beads arranged in ancient geometric motifs like the tina-uh (bamboo shoots) and linangkit (embroidery), which represent forest spirits and ancestral folklore. The silhouette is anchored by the Pipirot, a heavy ceremonial belt made from rows of silver coins and strands of red, yellow, and white beads that signify wealth and status. To finish the ensemble, women wear the Sinikot, a set of three elaborate hairpins often linked by silver chains that secure the hair in a traditional bun and symbolize the beauty of the Murut spirit.
